How can acting on renewable energy potential improve the ease of doing business in Sub-Saharan Africa?

One of the biggest obstacles to effectively growing an economy is a lack of access to affordable electricity. Unfortunately, Sub-Saharan Africa has some of the harshest economic and electrification conditions in the world. Luckily, there's a major opportunity in the form of renewable energy.

As mounting concerns about climate change and reliance on fossil fuels increase, so have the necessary technological advancements that allow for cost-efficient renewable infrustructure. And as turns out, some of the highest natural solar and wind potential fall on some of the least electrified Sub-Saharan African countries.

While there are many issues that come with not having proper access to electricity, I will focus on the ability to conduct business. There's certainly many ways to judge a country or a region's economic performance, but here we'll use the Ease of Doing Business Index. Created at the World Bank, the ease of doing business score and subsequent ranking means the "regulatory environment is more conducive to the starting and operation of a local firm." A higher ranking (low numerical value) indicate better, usually simpler, regulations for businesses and stronger protections of property rights. Empirical research funded by the World Bank shows that the economic growth impact of improving these regulations is strong.

The score is made up of a composite of 10 measurements weighted equally. You can read about it in more detail in the official report. One of the 10 critical metrics is "Getting Elecricity." Continue reading to learn more about the impact of electricity on local business and why the solution is in renewable energy.

Let's focus on a smaller subset

Less Than 25% Access to Electricity in Sub-Saharan Africa

13 Sub-Saharan African countries have less than 25% access to electricity. All of them also fall within the 30% hardest countries to do business in in the world, with an average ranking of 163 of 190.

Luckily, there's an opportunity. In many of these countries, there's some of the highest renewable potential in the entire world. Africa accounts for more solar irradiance than any other continent and in many countries exceeds the minimum requirements for wind power.
