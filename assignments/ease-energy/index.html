How can acting on renewable energy potential improve the ease of doing business in Sub-Saharan Africa?

By Andrew Levinson | November, 2018

One of the biggest obstacles to effectively growing an economy is a lack of access to affordable electricity. Unfortunately, Sub-Saharan Africa has some of the harshest economic and electrification conditions in the world, partially due to the fact that a traditional grid system isn't practical in most of rural Africa.

According to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), since 2010 the cost of solar PV technology has decreased by over 80% and wind power by about 50%. And as turns out, some of the highest natural solar and wind potential fall on some of the least electrified Sub-Saharan African countries.

As a measure of economic potential, we'll use the Ease of Doing Business Index. Created at the World Bank based on a composite of 10 critical factors1, a more favorable ranking indicates better, simpler, regulations for businesses, stronger protections of property rights, and affordable access to electricity. Research funded by the World Bank shows that the economic growth impact of improving these regulations is strong.
We'll explore the impact of electricity on local business and why the solution is in renewable energy.

Sub-Saharan Africa

Sub-Saharan Africa is spread out across the board, but the linear relationship is clearly present here. The more electrified a country is, the easier is it to do business.

Let's focus on a smaller subset

Less Than 25% Access to Electricity in Sub-Saharan Africa

13 Sub-Saharan African countries have less than 25% access to electricity. All of them also fall within the 30% hardest countries to do business in in the world, with an average ranking of 163 of 190.

Luckily, there's an opportunity. In many of these countries, there's some of the highest renewable potential in the entire world. Africa accounts for more solar irradiance than any other continent and in many countries exceeds the minimum requirements for wind power.
